# How to Optimize Your LinkedIn Profile

Most LinkedIn profiles are a resume pasted into a web form. That's a missed opportunity — LinkedIn isn't just a static document, it's a search surface recruiters actively query, a place where your network sees your activity, and often the first thing an interviewer looks at before ever opening your resume. Optimizing it is a different exercise from writing a resume, even though the raw material overlaps.

## Your headline is prime real estate

The default headline is your current job title. That's the least interesting thing you could put there. Your headline shows up everywhere — search results, comments, connection requests — and it's your chance to say more than a title in the same space. Instead of "Product Manager at Acme," consider something like "Product Manager | B2B SaaS | Turning user research into roadmaps that ship." Name your specialty, not just your role.

Recruiters also search LinkedIn by keyword, and the headline is heavily weighted in that search — so include the terms someone looking for your next role would actually type in.

## The About section should tell a story, not list facts

Your About section is one of the only places on LinkedIn written in your own voice, in full sentences. Use it. A strong About section:

- Opens with what you do and who you do it for, in one or two sentences
- Gives a brief arc of how you got here — the throughline, not a full chronology
- Names two or three concrete strengths or achievements
- Closes with what you're looking for or open to, if relevant

Avoid writing it in the third person ("Jane is a marketing leader...") — first person reads as more human and is the LinkedIn norm.

## Experience bullets: outcomes, not duties

The same principle that applies to resume bullets applies here, arguably even more so, since LinkedIn experience entries are often skimmed even faster than a resume. Lead with outcomes and impact, not a list of responsibilities. "Managed social media accounts" tells a reader nothing about whether you were good at it. "Grew organic social following 3x in 8 months through a content strategy overhaul" does.

## Skills and endorsements

LinkedIn's skills section directly powers a large part of its internal search — recruiters filter by skill constantly. List the skills that are actually true and actually relevant to the roles you want, not every skill you've ever touched. Pin your top three most important skills to the top of the section, since those are the ones shown by default.

Endorsements matter less than most people assume for search ranking, but a profile with zero endorsements on its top skills can read as under-maintained. A few genuine endorsements from real colleagues go a long way.

## Recommendations carry real weight

A written recommendation from a former manager or close collaborator is one of the strongest trust signals on the platform — it's third-party validation, not self-reported. If you don't have any, it's worth reaching out to two or three people you worked closely with and asking directly, ideally with a short reminder of a specific project they could speak to.

## Activity and consistency

A profile that hasn't posted, commented, or engaged in over a year can look dormant even if the content itself is strong. You don't need to post constantly — even occasional, genuine engagement (a thoughtful comment, a share with your own take added) keeps the profile looking active, which matters both to recruiters browsing profiles and to LinkedIn's own algorithm when it comes to how visible your profile is in search and your network's feed.

## Custom URL and photo

Two small, easy fixes many people skip: set a custom LinkedIn URL (linkedin.com/in/yourname instead of a string of numbers) so it looks clean on a resume or business card, and use a genuinely professional, friendly, well-lit headshot — profiles with a photo receive dramatically more views than those without one.

## Summary

- Your headline should include a specialty and searchable keywords, not just your title.
- Write the About section in first person, as a short narrative, not a bullet list.
- Experience bullets should show outcomes and impact, not just duties.
- Curate your skills list deliberately, and pin your top three.
- Ask for a few genuine written recommendations — they're a strong trust signal.
- Keep the profile lightly active, and don't skip the custom URL and a real photo.

A well-optimized LinkedIn profile does work for you passively — showing up in the right searches, giving the right first impression — long after you've stopped actively job hunting.
