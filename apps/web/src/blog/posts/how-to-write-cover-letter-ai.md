# How to Write a Cover Letter with AI (Without It Sounding Generic)

The complaint about AI-written cover letters is almost always the same: they sound generic. That's a real risk, but it's a risk that comes from how the tool is used, not something inherent to AI-assisted writing. A cover letter generated from good input and then lightly edited by a human can be genuinely strong — and dramatically faster than starting from a blank page.

## What a cover letter actually needs to do

Before getting into the AI part, it's worth being clear on the job a cover letter does that a resume doesn't:

- Explains *why this role, specifically* — not just that you're qualified in general
- Connects two or three of your strongest, most relevant achievements to what the role actually needs
- Shows a bit of genuine motivation or context a resume's bullet points can't carry
- Demonstrates that you can write clearly, which matters directly for roles involving communication

A cover letter that just restates your resume in paragraph form fails at all four of these, whether it was written by AI or by hand.

## Give the AI real input, not just a job title

The single biggest driver of a generic-sounding AI cover letter is generic input. If you give a tool nothing but "write me a cover letter for a marketing manager role," you'll get exactly what that prompt deserves — something that could apply to any marketing manager role at any company.

Instead, give it:

- **The actual job description**, not just the title — the specific responsibilities and requirements matter
- **Your actual resume or relevant experience**, so it's pulling real achievements rather than inventing plausible-sounding ones
- **A tone**, if you have a preference — more formal, more conversational, more concise

The more specific and real the input, the more specific and real the output.

## Let AI handle the structure, not the substance

AI is genuinely good at the parts of cover letter writing that are mechanical: producing a clean opening, organizing your achievements into a coherent paragraph flow, matching a requested tone, and avoiding the blank-page problem entirely. Treat the first draft as a strong structural starting point, not a finished letter.

## Edit for specificity and voice

This is the step people skip, and it's the one that actually prevents the "sounds generic" problem. After generating a draft:

1. **Add one detail only you would know** — something about the company, the team, or the role that shows you actually researched it, not just processed the job posting.
2. **Cut anything that could apply to any company.** If a sentence would work unchanged in a cover letter for a competitor, tighten it until it's specific to this one.
3. **Read it out loud.** If it doesn't sound like something you'd actually say, adjust the phrasing — even a strong draft can carry a slightly stiff, AI-generated cadence in a few sentences.
4. **Check the achievements are accurate**, not just plausible. AI drafting from a resume should stay grounded in what's actually there, but always verify specifics like numbers and dates before sending.

## A cover letter isn't a longer resume

One structural mistake that both AI drafts and human-written first drafts commonly make: walking through your work history chronologically, the way a resume does. A cover letter should instead pick two or three of your strongest, most relevant points and go deeper on those — not attempt comprehensive coverage.

## Summary

- A cover letter's job is to explain *why this role*, not restate your resume — AI or not.
- Feed the AI real input: the actual job description and your actual resume, not just a title.
- Let AI handle structure and the blank-page problem; you handle specificity and voice.
- Always add one detail only you would know, and cut anything generic enough to apply anywhere.
- Verify any numbers or specifics the draft includes before sending.

Used well, AI turns cover letter writing from a dreaded, time-consuming task into a fifteen-minute editing pass — which usually means you're more likely to actually write a tailored one for every application, rather than reusing the same generic letter everywhere.
