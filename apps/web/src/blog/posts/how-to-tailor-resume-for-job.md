# How to Tailor Your Resume for a Specific Job

Sending the same resume to every job is one of the most common reasons strong candidates never hear back. Recruiters and applicant tracking systems (ATS) are both looking for evidence that you fit *this* role, not a generic version of your career. Tailoring your resume to the job description is the single highest-leverage thing you can do before hitting submit.

This guide walks through exactly how to do it — in about ten minutes per application once you have the process down.

## Why generic resumes fail

A one-size-fits-all resume fails for two separate reasons:

- **ATS filtering.** Most mid-size and large companies screen applications through an ATS before a human ever sees them. These systems score your resume against the keywords in the job posting. A resume written in your own vocabulary, rather than the employer's, can score low even when your experience is a genuine match.
- **Human skimming.** Recruiters spend seconds, not minutes, on an initial resume scan. If the most relevant parts of your background aren't immediately visible near the top, they're easy to miss — even by a reader who would otherwise be impressed.

Tailoring fixes both problems at once: it raises your keyword match score and it puts your most relevant experience where a skimming reader will actually see it.

## Step 1: Analyse the job description

Before you touch your resume, read the job posting closely and pull out three things:

1. **The hard requirements** — specific tools, technologies, certifications, or years of experience listed as "required."
2. **The responsibilities** — the actual verbs used to describe the job ("lead," "own," "design," "coordinate"). These tell you how the employer frames the role internally.
3. **Repeated language** — terms that show up more than once. Repetition is a strong signal of what the employer's ATS is likely weighted to look for.

Keep this list next to your resume as you edit.

## Step 2: Match your keywords

Go through your resume's summary, skills section, and experience bullets, and check each requirement from Step 1 against your existing content. If you have the skill or experience but described it differently, rewrite it using the employer's exact terminology where it's honest to do so. If the posting says "stakeholder management" and your resume says "worked with cross-functional teams," align the language — you're describing the same thing, and the ATS shouldn't have to guess.

This is different from stuffing your resume with unrelated keywords, which usually backfires by making your resume read as generic rather than targeted. The goal is accurate translation, not keyword padding.

## Step 3: Reframe your experience, don't invent it

Tailoring is about emphasis and framing, not fabrication. For each role on your resume, ask: *which of my achievements are most relevant to this specific posting?* Reorder your bullet points so the most relevant ones for this job come first within each role. If you have five bullets under a past position and only two are relevant to the job you're applying for, lead with those two.

Where possible, quantify the achievement in terms the employer will recognise — if the job is about reducing costs, lead with a cost-reduction number; if it's about growth, lead with a growth number, even if that same accomplishment could be framed multiple ways.

## Step 4: Adjust your summary

Your resume's summary or headline is the first thing read, and it's the easiest place to lose a tailoring opportunity by leaving it generic. Rewrite it — even briefly — to name the target role or the core skill the posting is built around. A summary that says "Product manager with 6 years of experience in B2B SaaS, focused on onboarding and activation" is dramatically more effective for an onboarding-focused PM role than a generic "Experienced product manager who ships great products."

## Summary

- Generic resumes lose to both ATS filters and human skimmers.
- Read the job description first and extract required skills, key verbs, and repeated terms.
- Align your existing experience with the employer's language — don't invent new experience.
- Reorder bullets so the most relevant achievements for *this* job come first.
- Rewrite your summary for every application; it's the highest-visibility, lowest-effort tailoring win.
