# How to Pass ATS Resume Screening

If you've applied to dozens of jobs that match your background and heard nothing back, an applicant tracking system (ATS) may be the reason — not a human rejecting you, but software filtering you out before a human ever looks. The good news: most ATS rejections come down to a handful of fixable issues.

## What an ATS actually does

An ATS is software that employers use to collect, sort, and score incoming applications. Contrary to the popular myth, most modern ATS platforms don't "reject" resumes outright based on formatting alone — but they do parse your resume into structured fields (contact info, work history, skills, education) and often score it against the job requirements. A resume that parses badly, or that's missing the language the ATS is scanning for, ends up ranked low enough that a recruiter never reaches it in their queue.

## Common ATS mistakes

**Overly complex formatting.** Multi-column layouts, text boxes, tables, and graphics-heavy templates can parse incorrectly — your work history might get scrambled or dropped entirely. Stick to a clean, single-column layout with standard section headings.

**Headers and footers.** Some ATS platforms don't reliably read text placed in a document header or footer. If your contact information lives there, it may not get captured at all.

**Non-standard section titles.** "My Journey" instead of "Experience," or "What I Bring" instead of "Skills," can confuse an ATS's parsing logic, which is often built around expected section names.

**Missing keywords.** This is the big one. If the job posting requires "Salesforce" and your resume says "CRM software" without ever naming the specific tool, you can lose points on a match you're actually qualified for.

**Images and icons for contact info.** A phone icon next to your number looks nice to a human, but an ATS reading raw text can't extract a phone number from an image.

**File format issues.** PDF is generally safe with modern ATS platforms, but a scanned image saved as a PDF isn't — there's no actual text for the system to read. Always export from a real document, not a scan.

## How to build an ATS-friendly resume

1. **Use a standard, single-column layout** with clear section headings: Summary, Experience, Education, Skills.
2. **Put contact information in the main body** of the document, not in a header/footer.
3. **Use exact keyword matches** from the job description wherever they honestly apply — both the full term and common abbreviations (e.g. "Search Engine Optimization (SEO)").
4. **Spell out both acronyms and full terms** at least once, since you don't know which version the ATS is matching against.
5. **List skills explicitly** in a dedicated Skills section, rather than only implying them through experience bullets — some ATS scoring weighs an explicit skills list heavily.
6. **Save as PDF from a text-based source** (a resume builder or word processor), never a scanned image.
7. **Avoid tables for layout.** Tables are frequently mis-parsed, with content in one cell read out of order relative to another.

## Keywords without keyword stuffing

The instinct after learning about ATS keyword matching is often to cram in every relevant term possible. Don't. Beyond looking unnatural to the human reader who eventually sees your resume, some newer ATS platforms and most recruiters can detect keyword stuffing, and it reads as a red flag rather than a strength. The goal is accurate, natural coverage of the terms the job actually requires — not maximum density.

A practical test: read your resume out loud. If a sentence sounds like it was written for a machine rather than a person, rewrite it.

## Summary

- ATS platforms parse and score your resume before a human sees it — clean formatting and accurate keywords are what get you through.
- Avoid multi-column layouts, headers/footers for contact info, tables, and image-based text.
- Match the job description's language, including both acronyms and full terms.
- Keyword coverage should be honest and natural, not stuffed.
- Test your resume against real job descriptions before you rely on it for a real application.

Getting past the ATS isn't about tricking software — it's about making sure the system can actually read what's already true about your background.
